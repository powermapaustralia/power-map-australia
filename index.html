Purpose

Australian policy often creates winners and losers. This project makes the incentive structure visible: donors, companies, industries, parties, policy areas, possible private benefit and possible public cost.

Legal-safe rule: the site should say “may benefit”, “has exposure to”, “policy alignment”, and “raises conflict-of-interest questions” unless direct evidence proves stronger claims.

Methodology

How profiles are built

1. Disclosed money

Start with AEC disclosure data. Record donor, recipient, amount, date/year and source URL.

2. Business interests

Map companies, ownership, industries, investments, government contracts and policy exposure.

3. Policy alignment

Identify policies that plausibly affect those interests: tax, approvals, energy, migration, defence, procurement and industrial relations.

4. Benefit test

Balance public benefits against public costs. Jobs and tax matter. So do subsidies, environmental costs, housing pressure and influence concentration.

Scoring model

Australia benefit test

Public benefit indicators

  • Local jobs and wages
  • Tax and royalties
  • Productive infrastructure
  • Export earnings
  • Innovation or capability

Public cost indicators

  • Subsidies and procurement risk
  • Environmental externalities
  • Housing or migration pressure
  • Market concentration
  • Political influence imbalance
