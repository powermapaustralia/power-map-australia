Legal standard

This site avoids unsupported claims of bribery or corruption. It documents disclosed donations, ownership interests, policy exposure and reasonable conflict-of-interest questions.

What this tracks

Political power is not only about speeches. It is also about incentives: who pays, who lobbies, who owns assets, what policies change, and who benefits.

What this does not claim

A donation is not proof that policy was bought. A business interest is not proof of wrongdoing. The purpose is to show relationships and let evidence do the work.

Method

How each profile is assessed

1. Donations

Use official AEC disclosure data where available. Record donor name, recipient, amount, year and source.

2. Interests

Map major ownership, corporate control, industries and assets from public filings, annual reports and reliable reporting.

3. Policy alignment

Identify policies that plausibly affect those interests: tax, regulation, approvals, migration, defence, energy and procurement.

4. Australia test

Separate claimed public benefits from possible public costs. Jobs and tax count. So do subsidies, externalities and concentration of influence.

Wording rule: use “may benefit”, “has exposure to”, “policy alignment”, “raises conflict-of-interest questions”. Avoid “bribery”, “bought policy”, or “corrupt” unless a court, regulator or primary evidence establishes it.
